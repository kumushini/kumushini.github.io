Exploring Joint Visual Attention in Natural and Collaborative Settings

Affiliation: Old Dominion University (August 2024 - Present)

This project investigates how individuals coordinate their visual attention during real world social and collaborative interactions. Using egocentric eye tracking data from multi user environments, we develop computational methods to detect and analyze joint visual attention, which occurs when multiple participants focus on the same object or region. Our approach models gaze behavior using spatiotemporal attention representations and deep learning based feature mapping to identify shared attention patterns across different tasks. By comparing collaborative and independent activities, the project examines how interaction context influences attention coordination and provides insights relevant to human computer interaction, behavioral analysis, and social robotics.

Investigating Device-Dependent Mental Effort in Digital Reading

Affiliation: Old Dominion University (March 2025 - Present)

In this project, we investigate how different digital reading devices influence the mental effort involved in reading. Using the Reading in the Wild dataset collected with Project Aria, we analyze eye-tracking data from reading tasks performed on four common devices: laptops, tablets, mobile phones, and wall-mounted screens. By examining fixation duration as a key eye-tracking metric, the study explores how ergonomic factors such as viewing distance and display size affect visual processing and cognitive effort during digital reading. The findings provide insights into device-dependent reading behaviors and contribute to the design of more user-friendly and cognitively efficient digital reading environments.

[Re] Network Deconvolution

Affiliation: Old Dominion University (February 2024 - May 2025)

This is a reproducibility study of Network Deconvolution; the original work proposed by Ye et al. (2020). The study is conducted to evaluate the ability to reproduce the results with current library dependencies, given that five years have passed since the original publication. The project requires Python GPU-based processing capabilities, TensorFlow and PyTorch frameworks.

DeepDDQ: Automated DNA Damage Quantification

Affiliation: Old Dominion University (February 2025 - April 2025)

In this short-term graduate course project, we developed DeepDDQ, a deep learning–based pipeline for automated quantification of DNA damage foci in fluorescence microscopy images. By integrating a fine-tuned CellPose 3.1 model for nuclei segmentation with extended-maxima–based foci detection, we demonstrated that the workflow reliably captures biologically relevant repair kinetics following irradiation.

Analyzing Transportation Mode Interactions

Affiliation: University of Peradeniya (February 2023 - December 2023)

This project reviews the use of agent based models for simulating traffic in urban environments to better understand congestion, mobility patterns, and sustainability challenges. By analyzing research studies and case examples published between 2014 and 2022, the study evaluates how agent based approaches are used to model traffic behavior and micro mobility systems. The work also highlights commonly used simulation tools, discusses data requirements, and identifies future research directions for improving urban traffic modeling and sustainable transportation planning.

Quantification of Landside Congestion in Ports

Affiliation: University of Peradeniya (September 2021 - December 2023)

This project analyzes truck related congestion at the Port of Colombo using GPS data from hinterland transport operations. By examining truck movement patterns and travel times, the study identifies congestion hotspots and time periods with the highest delays. The findings provide valuable insights for improving port infrastructure, traffic management, and logistics efficiency in port and hinterland transport systems.
